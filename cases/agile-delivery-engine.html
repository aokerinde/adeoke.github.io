01 · The Delivery Challenge

Predictability across a matrixed team, not just a backlog

The claims and compliance automation program spanned data science (model development), engineering (RPA and platform integration), compliance/audit (sampling and regulatory sign-off), and claims operations (day-to-day users) — four groups with different cadences, different definitions of "done," and priorities that shifted as regulatory guidance and model performance data came in. The risk wasn't a lack of ideas; it was a backlog that could look busy sprint over sprint without actually being predictable, because dependencies between workstreams weren't visible until they blocked someone.

Running this as a single delivery engine — one backlog, explicit cross-team dependencies, and a consistent ceremony cadence — is what turned four groups with different rhythms into a program that hit 97% sprint completion and improved velocity 20%, rather than four teams periodically waiting on each other.

02 · Backlog & Dependency Structure

Making cross-team blockers visible before they block anyone

Every story carried an explicit dependency tag back to the workstream it needed input from. Instead of discovering a blocker in standup, the backlog itself showed which stories were waiting on a model version, a compliance sign-off, or an engineering platform change.

04 · Sample Stories With Mockups

From story to shipped screen

These are three of the 150+ stories from the backlog, shown with the acceptance criteria that shipped and a lo-fi wireframe of the resulting screen — the same artifact I'd bring into sprint planning or a stakeholder review to ground a story in what the user would actually see.

STORY-042Must
Depends on: STORY-038 (risk-scoring API contract)

As a Claims Examiner, I want a single review queue that shows claims sorted by risk score and SLA deadline with the risk level visible at a glance, so that I always work the highest-priority claim next without cross-referencing another system.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given claims are in the queue, when it renders, then each row shows claim ID, member, risk badge, and SLA countdown.
  • Given a claim is within 24 hours of SLA, when displayed, then its SLA countdown is visually distinct from claims with more time remaining.

STORY-057Must
Depends on: STORY-051 (model explainability output)

As a Claims Examiner, I want a flagged claim's risk factors explained in plain language rather than raw model scores, so that I can quickly understand why a claim was flagged before I make a decision.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a claim is flagged, when the examiner opens it, then the top 3 contributing factors are shown with a plain-language description and relative weight.
  • Given the examiner reviews the factors, when they disagree, then they can log an override with an optional reason code.

05 · Velocity & Predictability Outcomes

What the dependency-first approach changed

Once dependencies were visible in the backlog instead of surfacing mid-sprint, sprint completion stabilized and velocity climbed as the team spent less time re-planning around late-discovered blockers.

Why dependency tagging mattered more than velocity tracking itself: tracking velocity tells you predictability is a problem after the fact. Tagging dependencies at the story level surfaced the same risk two to three sprints earlier, while there was still time to resequence work instead of just reporting a miss.
