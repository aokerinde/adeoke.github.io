01 · Business Background

Why claims and compliance, why now

Claims and back-office audit workflows relied on manual review at nearly every step: intake validation, code and eligibility checks, fraud/anomaly screening, adjudication, and compliance sampling were each performed by hand or with disconnected point tools. As claims volume grew, cycle times and error rates grew with it, and audit coverage was limited by how many claims a compliance team could physically sample. Regulatory expectations — timely adjudication SLAs, documentation standards, and fair, explainable decisioning — didn't move, but the manual process was increasingly the bottleneck to meeting them consistently.

AI and RPA were viable here specifically because the failure modes were well understood: most claims are routine and rules-based (good RPA fit), a minority carry fraud/anomaly risk that benefits from pattern detection at scale (good ML fit), and the remainder need human judgment (good human-in-the-loop fit). The PRD below reflects that three-way split rather than treating automation as an all-or-nothing replacement for claims examiners.

02 · Problem Statement

What was actually broken

Claims examiners and compliance auditors were spending the majority of their time on work that didn't require their judgment — manual data validation, re-keying information already available in source systems, and auditing claims essentially at random rather than by risk — while genuinely complex or high-risk claims didn't reliably get more scrutiny than routine ones.

  • Long, inconsistent cycle times — claims sat in queue not because they were complex, but because they were waiting behind simpler claims for the same manual validation steps.
  • Error rates driving rework — data-entry and eligibility mismatches weren't caught until later in the process, producing appeals, reprocessing, and provider abrasion.
  • Audit coverage was a sampling problem, not a rigor problem — compliance could only manually review a small, largely random slice of claims, so risk wasn't necessarily where the scrutiny was.
  • No consistent audit trail — reconstructing why a claim was adjudicated a certain way required piecing together notes across systems, which slowed both internal QA and external regulatory response.

04 · Scope

What Phase 1 includes — and deliberately excludes

Scope was deliberately constrained to routine, high-volume claim types first, so the team could prove the automation and governance model before extending it to more complex claim categories.

In scope — Phase 1

  • RPA validation of eligibility, provider network status, and code pairing at intake
  • AI risk/anomaly scoring on all validated claims
  • Human-in-the-loop review queue prioritized by risk and SLA
  • Immutable audit trail across automated and human decision points
  • Risk-stratified compliance sampling (replacing purely random sampling)
  • Operational dashboard for cycle time, error rate, and automation rate

Out of scope — Phase 1

  • Fully automated adjudication with no human review, for any claim tier
  • Appealed or previously-denied claims (routed to existing appeals process)
  • Non-standard/complex claim types with insufficient historical data to train a reliable model
  • Provider-facing self-service status tooling (handled by a separate initiative)

07 · Epics & User Stories

Backlog detail, with acceptance criteria

This build shipped from a backlog of 150+ user stories with acceptance criteria and dependencies. Below is a representative set across the four epics that structured the work — enough to show story-writing and acceptance-criteria style, not the full backlog. Priority tags use MoSCoW.

Epic AAutomated Claims Intake & Validation
RPA validates eligibility, network status, and coding before a claim ever reaches a human queue.
A1Must

As a Claims Operations Lead, I want incoming claims automatically validated against member eligibility, provider network status, and coding rules before entering the adjudication queue, so that claims with data-entry or eligibility errors are caught before they consume examiner time.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a claim is submitted electronically, when it enters the intake queue, then the RPA bot validates member ID, provider NPI, and procedure/diagnosis code pairing against reference data within 60 seconds.
  • Given a claim fails validation, when the failure is logged, then it is routed to an exception queue with a specific failure reason code, not a generic rejection.
  • Given a claim passes validation, when it is queued for adjudication, then an audit-log entry records the automated checks performed and their results.
A2Must

As a Claims Examiner, I want auto-validated claims to arrive pre-populated with eligibility and coding confirmation, so that I don't manually re-verify data the system has already checked.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a claim has passed RPA validation, when it appears in the examiner's queue, then eligibility status, network status, and code-pairing confirmation are displayed inline.
  • Given the examiner opens a claim, when they review it, then they can see a timestamped log of which checks were automated vs. manually reviewed.
A3Should

As a Compliance Auditor, I want RPA validation exception rates tracked by claim type and reason code, so that I can identify systemic data-quality issues upstream rather than one-off errors.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given validation exceptions occur, when they are logged, then they are categorized by reason code and claim type in a reporting table.
  • Given a reason code exceeds a defined exception-rate threshold in a rolling 7-day window, then an alert is generated for the compliance team.
Epic BAI-Powered Anomaly & Fraud Detection
Every validated claim is risk-scored so scrutiny concentrates where the risk actually is.
B1Must

As a Product Manager, I want the AI model to score every validated claim for anomaly/fraud risk before it reaches an examiner, so that high-risk claims get additional scrutiny without slowing down low-risk, routine claims.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a claim passes intake validation, when it is scored, then the model returns a risk score (0–100) and top contributing factors within 5 seconds.
  • Given a claim's risk score exceeds the defined threshold, when it is routed, then it is flagged for mandatory secondary review rather than standard adjudication.
  • Given the model flags a claim, when an examiner reviews it, then the top contributing factors are shown in plain language, not raw model output.
B2Must

As a Data Scientist, I want model predictions logged with the feature values used at scoring time, so that I can audit and retrain the model against ground-truth outcomes.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a claim is scored, when the score is generated, then the feature vector, model version, and score are persisted to the model logging store.
  • Given a claim's final adjudication outcome is recorded, when it differs from the model's flag, then the discrepancy is captured for retraining analysis.
B3Should

As an AI Governance Lead, I want fairness and bias metrics computed on model outputs across protected classes on a recurring basis, so that we can detect and correct disparate impact before it becomes a compliance issue.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a rolling window of scored claims, when the fairness job runs weekly, then disparity metrics (e.g., false-positive rate parity) are computed across defined demographic segments.
  • Given a disparity metric exceeds the defined tolerance, when detected, then it is escalated to the AI Governance review board before the next model deployment.
B4Could

As a Claims Examiner, I want to provide feedback when I disagree with a model's risk flag, so that the feedback can be used to improve future model versions.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given an examiner overrides a model flag, when they submit their decision, then they can optionally record a reason code for the override.
  • Given override feedback is collected, when a model retraining cycle begins, then override data is included in the training/evaluation dataset.
Epic CHuman-in-the-Loop Review & Adjudication
Examiners work a single prioritized queue with full context and a documented decision trail.
C1Must

As a Claims Examiner, I want a unified review queue that prioritizes claims by risk score and SLA deadline, so that I work on the claims that matter most first.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given multiple claims are in the queue, when it is rendered, then claims are sorted by a combination of SLA due date and risk score, with high-risk/near-SLA claims surfaced first.
  • Given an examiner selects a claim, when they open it, then all relevant claim, member, and provider data is visible on a single screen without navigating to other systems.
C2Must

As a Claims Examiner, I want to approve, deny, or escalate a claim with a documented rationale, so that every adjudication decision is defensible in an audit.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given an examiner makes a decision, when they submit it, then a rationale (free text or structured reason code) is required before the decision is finalized.
  • Given a decision is submitted, when it is recorded, then it is immutable and timestamped in the audit trail.
C3Should

As a Claims Operations Manager, I want claims approaching SLA breach automatically escalated, so that no claim silently ages out of compliance.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a claim is within 24 hours of its SLA deadline and unassigned or unresolved, when the SLA monitor runs, then the claim is automatically escalated to a supervisor queue.
  • Given a claim is escalated, when it is reassigned, then the original examiner and new examiner are both notified.
Epic DAudit Trail, Compliance & Reporting
Every automated and human decision is logged, sampled, and reportable without manual reconstruction.
D1Must

As a Compliance Auditor, I want a complete, immutable audit trail for every claim showing every automated and human touchpoint, so that I can respond to regulatory audits without reconstructing history manually.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a claim moves through intake, scoring, review, and adjudication, when each step occurs, then it is logged with actor (system or user), timestamp, and action taken.
  • Given an auditor requests a claim's full history, when the audit trail is exported, then it includes every automated decision, model version used, and human override.
D2Must

As a Compliance Auditor, I want configurable, risk-stratified audit sampling, so that we maintain oversight without auditing every claim manually.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a defined sampling rate and stratification rule (e.g., by claim type, risk tier), when the sampling job runs, then it generates a representative sample for manual audit review.
  • Given a sampled claim is reviewed, when a discrepancy is found, then it is logged and rolled up into a monthly compliance report.
D3Should

As a Product Manager, I want a real-time operational dashboard showing cycle time, error rate, and automation rate by claim type, so that I can monitor whether the initiative is hitting its target metrics without pulling manual reports.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given claims are processed, when the dashboard refreshes, then average cycle time, error/rework rate, and % of claims auto-validated are shown by claim type, updated at least daily.
  • Given a metric trends outside its target range for more than 3 consecutive days, when the dashboard detects it, then an alert is sent to the product and operations leads.
D4Could

As an Executive Sponsor, I want a monthly summary report translating operational metrics into cost savings and ROI, so that I can report initiative impact to leadership without needing raw data access.

Acceptance Criteria
  • Given a calendar month closes, when the summary report generates, then it translates cycle-time and error-rate improvements into estimated cost savings using the agreed methodology.
  • Given the report is generated, when distributed, then it is delivered to defined stakeholders on a fixed monthly cadence.

These 14 stories are drawn from the full backlog of 150+ used to run this initiative; the remainder followed the same epic structure across additional claim types, edge cases, and reporting variants.

09 · Risks & Assumptions

What could go wrong, and the plan for it

RiskMitigation

10 · Rollout Plan

How this went from pilot to full scale

PhaseScopeExit criteria
Phase 1 — PilotSingle high-volume, low-complexity claim type; RPA validation + risk scoring in shadow mode (flagged but not yet routing)Model precision/recall meets threshold in shadow mode; RPA exception rate below target
Phase 2 — Live routingRisk-based routing goes live for the pilot claim type; audit sampling shifts to risk-stratifiedCycle-time and error-rate targets met for two consecutive months; zero critical audit findings
Phase 3 — ExpansionExtend to additional claim types; add examiner feedback loop into model retrainingConsistent metric performance across expanded claim types; sprint completion sustained ≥95%
Phase 4 — Steady stateFull operational dashboard, quarterly fairness review cadence, ongoing model retrainingOngoing — ownership transitions to steady-state operations with quarterly business reviews
Why phased, not big-bang: starting in shadow mode on one claim type let us validate model quality and RPA reliability against real production data without any risk to live adjudication — the same discipline of proving the riskiest assumption first that I apply across every initiative.
